01
User as Protagonist
The grower is the hero. ORTH is the infrastructure that enabled the outcome.
ORTH's AI detected early-stage rust pressure and triggered a preventive response.

02
Outcomes Over Capabilities
Describe consequences. Feature lists create cognitive load. Outcomes demonstrate value.

03
Specificity Over Abstraction
Ground claims in field reality. Vague claims are indistinguishable from competitors.

04
Demonstration Over Celebration
Show value through use. Restraint builds credibility. Self-congratulation erodes it.

Season two looked different from season one. The spray log was shorter. The yield was not.

Transparent Limitations
Acknowledge what ORTH cannot do. A low confidence score is not a warning. It is an invitation to provide more context. Honesty builds more trust than overpromising.

ORTH flagged the risk. The call on varietal resistance stayed with the agronomist.

One communication truth.

ORTH gets better the more context it has. The first session is not the full picture. External communications should set this expectation honestly: not as a disclaimer, but as part of how the product is described. The ramp is real and worth stating.

When the context demands more.
LinkedIn

LinkedIn allows for more presence. Posts can open with a specific field scenario, a grower result, or a concrete observation. A degree of narrative is appropriate. The tone stays grounded: no excitement, no buzzwords, but it can carry a stronger point of view and end with a clear statement of what ORTH does or believes.

Specific. Point-of-view. Ends with conviction.

Investor decks

Decks can state scale, ambition, and market opportunity directly. Numbers and claims belong here in a way they do not in product copy. The framing stays grounded: lead with the problem, show the evidence, make the case. But the register shifts toward investment logic: size, timing, defensibility.

Evidence-led. Ambitious but traceable.

Partner pitches

Partner-facing writing can be warmer and more direct about mutual benefit. State what ORTH does, what you need from the partner, and what the grower gains. Avoid soft language around collaboration and synergy. The most credible pitch is a specific problem, a concrete solution, and a shared outcome.

Direct. Mutual. Problem first.

Institutional and government

Enterprise and government contexts require a more measured register. Lead with scale, coverage, and reliability. Avoid product-forward language. Institutions are buying infrastructure, not a feature set. State what ORTH does across operations, what the agronomic outcome is at scale, and what the accountability model looks like. Specificity about geography and crop type builds credibility faster than any claim about AI.

Scale. Reliability. Outcome at the operation level.

Cocogoose anchors every display moment. Source Serif 4 introduces contrast. Nunito Sans handles everything else.

Cocogoose Pro Semilight
Primary
identity.

Every display heading, hero, section title, feature title, and stat number. Cocogoose is the visual anchor of the system. Its geometric weight and generous width give ORTH its presence at scale.

–Semilight (weight 300) only. No other weights exist in this system.
–No italic. Source Serif 4 is the system's contrast device. Cocogoose stays upright.
–No condensed variants.
–Minimum use size: 22px. Below this, switch to Nunito Sans.
–Letter-spacing: –0.04em at hero scale, –0.03em at feature scale.

Source Serif 4
One word.
Italic.

Source Serif 4 plays one role: the italic contrast word inside a Cocogoose headline. The serif against the geometric creates productive tension. It is never used for body text, subheads, or standalone copy.

–Italic only. The roman weight is not in use.
–One word per headline as a strong default. The italic should carry weight, not decoration. More than one and the tension collapses.
–Size: 1.1em of the parent Cocogoose element.
–Pick the word that carries the most meaning or tension. It should feel inevitable, not decorative, not random.

Every call has
a consequence.

Know what you're building. Know who depends on it.

An olive farm. Eastern Mediterranean.

The soil here is hard and full of stones. The land sat untouched for nearly forty years inside a buffer zone before anyone was allowed to cultivate it again. When Giorgos David received permission in 2012 and planted the first trees, he was making a bet on land that had no recent record: no history of yield, no data on pest pressure, no proof of what it could become.

Today, the farm produces one of the highest polyphenol olive oils in the world. Nobody fully understands why. It may be the location, where winds move through the valley from different directions. It may be the mineral-rich soil, influenced by a copper mine nearby. It may simply be the forty years of silence, the land resting and recovering before anyone asked anything of it.

Whatever the reason, the outcome belongs to the people who showed up every morning and made decisions under uncertainty.

Anastasia Panteli, 30.
Agriculturist.

She grew up in a family of growers. She always knew she wanted to work close to the land. Her day starts in the fields: irrigation, organic fertilization, the health of three olive varieties growing together across this stony ground.

"Never a day is the same in agriculture. There is always a new schedule for the next day."

What she means is: the conditions are never stable. The weather shifts. A pressure builds in one block that wasn't there last week. The window to act is often narrow, and reading it correctly depends on what you know and how fast you can know it.

"Every year is different. The weather changes, conditions change, and we have to adapt."

This is the environment ORTH is built for. The environment is a field in October where humidity moves differently across plots, where a grower with years of observation still needs confirmation before she commits to a decision. ORTH does not replace what Anastasia knows. It gives her a second signal, a pattern she might have missed, the forty minutes she needs to act while the window is still open.

She makes the call. She moves. The outcome is hers.

Maria Occaridou, 25.
Food scientist.

Her work begins where Anastasia's ends, at the point where the olives leave the field and enter the mill. She monitors temperature before processing, watches the crusher during extraction, and tracks quality from the first press to the final bottle. The oil extraction takes between one and one and a half hours. The margin for error is small.

"It makes me proud when our oil receives awards. But also when people tell us it helped them with health issues."

Maria's pride is specific. Her pride is in the chain of decisions, from the soil to the tree to the harvest to the extraction, being executed with enough care that the result is genuinely different from everything else on the shelf.

This is what trust looks like at the production level. Every decision upstream has a consequence downstream. If Anastasia misread the pressure in October, Maria would see it in the quality in November. The work is connected. The accountability is total.

ORTH lives in that chain. ORTH sits inside that chain making each decision slightly more informed, slightly more timely, slightly more confident. The grower still carries the weight. ORTH gives her better footing.

What this means for
how we speak.

Anastasia said something worth holding onto: sometimes agriculture feels like gambling. You think next year will be better, but something unexpected always happens. She said it with a smile. She is not defeated by uncertainty. She has learned to work inside it, to make her best decision with the information available, and to trust that the accumulation of good decisions over many seasons adds up to something.

That is the relationship ORTH is trying to support. A grower who is already capable, already committed, already carrying knowledge that no algorithm fully captures, and who benefits from a tool that is honest about what it knows and what it doesn't.

We do not write for people who need to be convinced that agriculture is important. We write for people who are already in the field before sunrise, already running the numbers, already making calls that nobody else will second-guess. They do not need inspiration. They need clarity.

So we give them that. We name the crop, the region, the pressure, the window. We say what the data shows and where it is uncertain. We let the grower decide.

And when we speak to the world about what ORTH is, we speak from this place. Not from a pitch. From a field where two young women help produce one of the most distinctive olive oils in the world, and where every good outcome traces back to a decision made on time, with the right information, by a person who knew what to do with it.

That person is who we are building this for.

Every word we put into the world should be worthy of her attention.

Anatomy
Mark

Organic closed loop. Roughly circular outer arc with an inner negative space that pulls to a soft triangle, apex at upper center. Uniform stroke weight throughout. Reads as circle and vessel simultaneously.

Wordmark

ORTH always all-caps. Geometric sans-serif. The O is a perfect circle, an intentional contrast with the organic mark. R, T, H carry rounded terminals with generous tracking.

Usage rules
Permitted
Use any of the four approved variants on their specified background type.
Scale uniformly from any corner. Proportions are locked.
Place on solid backgrounds: ink-950 through ink-800 for dark variants; stone-50 and white for light variants.
Place on photography when contrast is guaranteed and clear space is maintained.
Use the mark alone (without wordmark) in established contexts: app icon, favicon, social avatar.
Prohibited
Do not recolor the mark outside the four approved variants.
Do not stretch, skew, rotate, or distort the mark or lockup.
Do not add drop shadows, glows, gradients, or outlines.
Do not place a dark-background variant on a light background, or vice versa.
Do not recreate the wordmark in any typeface. Use approved SVG files only.
Do not place the logo on mid-tone surfaces, busy photography, or backgrounds that reduce contrast below legibility.
